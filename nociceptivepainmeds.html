Chronic Pain Management A Guide For Clinicians

Stepped Care Medications for Nociceptive Pain

(e.g., OA, not myofascial pain, fibromyalgia)

Intra-articular corticosteroid / topical NSAID / capsaicin: Diabetics should monitor glucose post-corticosteroid injection; prescribe assistive device. It is prudent to monitor Cr after topical NSAID initiated

Acetaminophen: 325 - 1,000 mg q4-8h, max 3 gm/d (ask about all OTCs with acetaminophen)

Salsalate: 500-750 mg bid (max3 gm/d) with food or milk (mild anti-inflammatory); does not interfere with platelet function; GI bleeding and nephrotoxicity rare, but Cr monitoring is prudent

Celecoxib: 100-200 mg bid. Not for long-term use in elderly

Ibuprofen 400mg po q4-6h OR naproxen 250-500 mg bid with food or milk. Do not use long-term in older adults. For any longterm use, add PPI/misoprostol

Remember, with NSAIDS to avoid in older adults, avoid in renal insufficiency, and use with extreme caution in heart failure and CHF

Duloxetine: Consider for musculoskeletal pain; 20-30mg/d; increase to 60mg/d in 7d. Not recommended in ESRD or CLcr< 30

Tramadol: Start 25mg qd; increase by 25-50 mg in divided doses every 3-7 days to max dose of 100mg QID. Renal dosing (CRcl < 30 ml/min) 100mg BID. Educate on s/sx of serotonin syndrome if other serotonergics

Hydrocodone/acetaminophen: 5/325–10/500 mg q4-6h; max acetaminophen dose 3gm/day

Oxycodone: 5-10 mg q4h OR morphine 2.5-5 mg q4h(not recommended if CrCl< 30); assess total needs after 7d on stable dose, then convert to long acting; if morphine needed, avoid long acting if renal insufficiency

Fentanyl and methadone: safest of opioids if renal insufficiency; ALWAYS consult pain expert
